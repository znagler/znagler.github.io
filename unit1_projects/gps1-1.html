Peer Pairing

  • Importance

What is it?

Peer pairing is the idea of two people working together on a single program or project. Typically one person navigates, meaning that they say exactly what to type, and the other person drives, or does the typing. These roles can be taken very strictly or more loosely. Often times both programmers are changing roles and sometimes stepping out of their assigned role to make suggestions.

A crucial part of pair prograaming is communication. Both parties need to be in agreement about their end goals and they need to be willing to ask questions and explain their reasoning so that both are comfortable about the direction the project is taking.

Why is it important?

Peer pairing is a great tool for programmers. It allows each party to learn the tricks and strategies that the other has picked up. This is especially important in DBC where there is so much material to cover. Learning from your peers is a great way to absorb concepts. It also simulates a real workplace, because a large component of software developing is reading and reviewing other people's code.

Peer pairing is surprisingly efficient. Since there is somebody working with you, you stay focused on the task and don't daze off. It allows for live debugging, and is also overall more enjoyable.

What are the challenges?

Although peer pairing has many benefits it does pose a few challenges. First of all are the technical challenges. This is true only of remote pairing, like we are doing in phase 0. Technical challenges include scheduling time when both people are available, using platforms like stypi that are occasionaly fussy and have to deal with last minute loss of internet connections.

Even in face-to-face pairing, there can still be challenges. Not everyone works well together and part of being a good pair is being willing to listen to your peer and respect their opinions and input. You need to be able to work well with others. This is true even beyond one person being a jerk. Sometimes people just have different working styles or thinking styles. Despite the challenges peer pairing is an essential part of programming.
